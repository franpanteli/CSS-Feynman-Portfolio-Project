About this project

This is a publication portfolio for Richard Feynman created by Fran Panteli for an exercise as part of a LinkedIn Learning CSS course on website design. This webpage is intended to be viewed from a computer browser as part of this exercise. I chose to create this portfolio for Richard Feynman, as he was a famous Physicist who first proposed the quantum computer with Yuri Manin in the 1980s and wish to celebrate some of his accomplishments within the modern context of website design. While this does contain information about Feynman, the primary focus of this exercise was to practice CSS and hence the technical details of his work were explored in little depth. His accomplishments have been mentioned and formatted here with the aim of practicing the CSS and HTML skills I have learnt from the LinkedIn Learning course called CSS Essential Training.

An American theoretical physicist

Richard Feynman graduated from Princeton University in 1942 and specialised in theoretical Physics. He was awarded six notable prizes throughout his lifetime, including the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1965. During this time, computers were professionals who performed calculations. Feynman managed a group of human computers during the conception of the first nuclear bomb (the Manhattan Project), a notable achievement of modern Physics. This was a key example in which computation aided in scientific discovery. Later, Feynman would discover new methods of computing logarithms that were based on the Connection Machine, and theorise the quantum computer. These accomplishments in scientific computing paved the way both for modern peacetime and for the furtherance of quantum computing in a modern context.

Three Notable Publications

These are three of Feynman's publications in an ordered (numbered) list. Two are books and the third is a written commentary Feynman gave on a theoretical Physics lecture. These publications first introduce the concepts of modern Physics (1) and then Feynman's work as a theoretical Physicist in exploring the limits of classical computing and hypothesising a quantum computer (2, 3).

  1. The Feynman Lectures on Physics
  2. Feynman Lectures On Computation (Frontiers in Physics)
  3. Simulating physics with computers

While this section included an h2 heading, these publications are next linked to and introduced in three smaller h3 headings. A decorative style for these headings was selected using Google fonts.

1. The Feynman Lectures on Physics

These are Feynman's introductory lectures on Physics and were presented for undergraduate students at the California Institue of Technnology from 1961-1963. This image includes an 'alt' attribute to make the text accessible.

2. Feynman Lectures On Computation (Frontiers in Physics)

These are Feynman's lectures on computation (1996). These lectures contain information about Turing machines (early quantum computers), the thermodynamics of computation and the quantum limits of computation. This work explores the limitations of digital computers from the perspective of a theoretical Physicist and is broadly accepted as ahead of its time.

Click to view: Feynman Lectures On Computation (Frontiers in Physics)

3. Simulating physics with computers

This publication is a paper written on a theoretical Physics lecture given by Feynamn in 1982. This discusses the limits of classical computation when exploring Physical systems which are quantum in nature.
